# Tending the world

*Notes toward a game in which making the world and living in it are one
activity.*

## The idea

Moppe need not choose between being a motorcycle game and being a terrain
laboratory.  Riding the world and working on the world can become two ways of
paying attention to the same thing.

The conventional division is that an editor produces a level and a game later
consumes it.  The player receives a finished object.  This is useful software
organization, but it need not be the structure of the experience.  A home is
not first made and then, in a separate phase, lived in.  Living reveals what
the home needs; repair, rearrangement, wear, memory, and care continue its
making.  A path is made in the same way.  Someone crosses the grass, another
person follows, rain objects, a better line is found, a crossing is improved,
and eventually the route has a name.

The game can be about **tending the world**: encountering a generated land,
learning what it is trying to become, and helping some of its possibilities
become durable places.

This does not retreat from procedural generation.  The generator remains the
first author, capable of making geology, water, and conjunctions no person
would have drawn.  Its purpose is not to manufacture disposable finished
levels.  It creates a world with more latent structure than either the system
or the player initially understands.  Play is the process by which that
structure is discovered, judged, and continued.

## One activity, several forms of attention

The motorcycle and Terrain Lab know different truths.

From the motorcycle, a place is temporal and bodily.  It is the rate at which
a view opens, the speed carried into a crest, the moment the wheels leave the
ground, the width of a forgiving landing, the line found only after several
failures, and the recognition of an approach ridden before.  No overhead
analysis can decide whether these things feel alive.

From above, the same place has a structure the rider cannot directly see:
basins, flow accumulation, alternative corridors, long profiles, catchments,
standing water, route hierarchies, and the effects one local edit may have
downstream.  No single ride can reveal all of these relationships.

These are not game mode and editor mode.  They are embodied and reflective
views of one world.  Moving between them should eventually feel more like
looking closely and stepping back than leaving the game to operate a tool.
The exact interface may still use windows, cameras, overlays, or pauses; the
important unity is causal.  What is learned in either view can become an act
in the shared world, and the consequence must be encountered again in both.

## The loop

The long game loop is not generate, consume, discard.  It is:

```text
encounter -> notice -> diagnose -> propose -> change -> inhabit -> remember
     ^                                                               |
     `---------------------------------------------------------------'
```

A player might repeatedly ride between a sheltered lake and a saddle.  The
wheels establish several faint traces.  One direct line becomes a wet rut;
another finds a stable bench.  The player marks that line, improves a stream
crossing, clears a view, and shapes an existing crest into a better takeoff.
Other movers adopt the route.  Its junction becomes a stopping place.  A tree,
cairn, bridge, or story makes it recognizable.  The next time the player
arrives, it is no longer merely a coordinate selected by an algorithm.

Not every step needs to be an explicit construction command.  Authorship can
occur at several levels:

- passage wears vegetation, compacts landings, and records desire;
- abandonment lets weak traces recover;
- water damages alignments that fight the slope;
- markers, names, and recorded lines turn attention into persistent values;
- local work clears, plants, drains, bridges, benches, or repairs;
- analytical views compare consequences and provisional futures;
- the player accepts, rejects, revises, or removes an intervention.

The smallest acts should come first.  A world that responds to use can begin
becoming authored before the game has a road-building interface.

## Structure-preserving play

Christopher Alexander's structure-preserving transformation is a useful
discipline for this activity.  A change should begin with the structure that
already exists and strengthen it while introducing something new.  It should
not treat the land as neutral material waiting for content.

A path along a bench can make the bench more legible.  A bridge at a narrow,
already meaningful crossing can strengthen river, banks, route, and arrival
at once.  A jump that develops a natural crest and lands with the descending
face can intensify the landform and the ride together.  A straight road cut
across several basins may function perfectly while destroying the centers it
passes through.

This is not a demand for realism or timidity.  Rockets, improbable jumps, and
licensed miracles can belong.  The question is whether an intervention
creates relationships or merely occupies space.  The magic mirror proposed
in the second-author notes succeeds if terrain, committing approach, flight,
view, and return all make it feel as if this particular mountain called for
it.

Each accepted change should ideally do three things:

1. form a coherent center in itself;
2. strengthen one or more centers already present;
3. help form a larger whole that neither old nor new center made alone.

That is a better design contract than maximizing a global beauty or fun
score.

## Instruments, not oracles

Bin Jiang's **Beautimeter** is superficially comic and fundamentally
interesting.  His mathematical work represents wholeness as recursively
supported centers and estimates structural beauty from the number and
hierarchy of substructures.  A later GPT-based Beautimeter inspects images
through Alexander's fifteen properties.  These approaches make a serious
wager: some beauty lies in discoverable relations among scales, boundaries,
symmetries, contrasts, gradients, and centers rather than in an inexplicable
finish applied by a gifted person.

Moppe should accept the wager without appointing a meter as judge.  A world
instrument might report that a new path strengthens a saddle but weakens a
lake boundary; that a landing is physically generous but has no readable
approach; or that added detail increases small-scale structure while reducing
inner calm.  It should expose several accounts which are allowed to disagree:

- structural hierarchy and support among centers;
- the fifteen geometrical properties;
- hydrological and ecological fit;
- ride trajectories, choices, failures, and recovery;
- visits, traces, names, and remembered events;
- pairwise human judgment from overhead and on the motorcycle.

The disagreement is the instrument's value.  A place can be visually strong
and hydrologically foolish.  A messy corner can be loved because of what
happened there.  A generated center can score well and feel like nothing when
visited.  The human remains the wholeness evaluator, but judgment becomes
better informed and leaves a reproducible history.

Instead of asking for a percentage, the useful questions compare concrete
futures:

- Which route seems more truly part of this land?
- Which change makes the lake more itself?
- Which jump would you want to try again?
- Which version makes the larger region easier to remember?
- What did this intervention strengthen, and what did it silence?

The answers become the next stratum of the world.

## Partial precedents in games

No one of these games is the model for Moppe.  Each has discovered part of
the territory.

**Death Stranding** makes traversal itself consequential.  Repeated passage
wears paths through difficult ground; ladders, ropes, bridges, and roads left
by one player enter other players' worlds.  The important achievement is not
construction breadth but the conversion of a journey from private
consumption into inherited infrastructure.  Moppe can take the quieter part
further: traces contested by water and vegetation, routes promoted by use,
and places changed by how they are approached.

**Eco** puts construction inside a reactive ecosystem.  Individual and
collective acts consume resources and alter an underlying simulation, so
building cannot be cleanly separated from stewardship.  Its political and
economic scale differs from Moppe, but it shows how diagnosis and consequence
can be play rather than an editor's concern.

**Terra Nil** makes restoration the main verb of a builder.  Its pleasure is
in reading a damaged landscape, restoring water and ecological succession,
and eventually withdrawing machinery.  It is a finite puzzle of repair;
Moppe's corresponding possibility is an open-ended inhabited landscape in
which repair, use, and new damage continue together.

**Wildmender** joins exploration to gardening, canals, planting, terrain
shaping, and the gradual growth of an oasis.  It demonstrates that tending a
world can support adventure and progression rather than merely decorate a
home base.

**Wurm Online** is valuable as a record of persistence.  Players terraform,
build settlements and infrastructure, and leave histories in a shared world
that outlasts individual sessions.  Its complexity also warns that a wide
construction vocabulary does not by itself make acts meaningful; the world
must answer back clearly enough for care and judgment to form.

**Townscaper** shows a gentler partnership with an algorithm.  A tiny human
gesture is elaborated by contextual rules into stairs, arches, courtyards,
bridges, and roofs.  The person does not specify every part, and the generator
does not decide the town.  That division of labor is close to what Moppe
could seek for paths and places: the player indicates and judges; the system
articulates and adapts.

**Cloud Gardens** makes composition out of the negotiation between plants and
discarded structures.  It is a small aesthetic toy, but its pleasure comes
from neither pure natural simulation nor unconstrained placement.  The player
sets conditions and recognizes a moment when the mutual transformation feels
complete.

**Riders Republic** is a useful production-side relative.  Ubisoft generated
large trail networks by fitting algorithmic paths to terrain and then shaping
them for different sports and gameplay.  Moppe's opportunity is to bring that
work inside the fiction and the play: route discovery, validation, revision,
and eventual construction performed by the world's inhabitants rather than
finished before they arrive.

Minecraft, gardening games, settlement sandboxes, track editors, and creative
toys form a wider background.  The distinguishing ambition here is not that
players may modify terrain.  It is that geology, hydrology, movement, human
judgment, and accumulated memory all participate in deciding which
modifications become places.

## A world history rather than a finished level

The current `TerrainProgram` is a reproducible account of how terrain was
made.  A later world value could continue that account beyond generation:

```text
geological source
  -> erosion and lakes
  -> first journeys
  -> trail emergence
  -> crossing reinforced
  -> old path damaged by water
  -> replacement adopted
  -> landing shaped
  -> place named
  -> later habitation
```

Reproducibility does not mean the world was planned in advance.  It means its
unfolding can be remembered, inspected, replayed, and understood.  The seed
creates possibility; simulation proposes consequences; the player selects a
history.

This gives persistence its proper role.  A procedural world is often
disposable because neither generator nor player remains in conversation with
it after creation.  Another seed is always cheaper than attention.  A tended
world reverses that value: generation is abundant, but this world's history
is irreplaceable.

## Shape of the work

This direction does not require an immediate grand construction system.  It
suggests an order of small proofs:

1. record player passages, takeoffs, landings, braking, and repeated lines;
2. render those traces as readings before they change geometry;
3. let vegetation and surface marks respond reversibly to sustained use;
4. expose candidate centers and route alternatives in the shared world;
5. allow markers, names, and blessed lines to persist;
6. add one local act of repair or intensification with visible consequences;
7. compare provisional transformations from above and from the motorcycle;
8. retain the accepted sequence as world history;
9. introduce other movers and longer ecological feedback only after the
   first acts are worth caring about.

The guardrails remain those of the terrain work: readings before laws,
observation separate from mutation, explicit values, honest ledgers, small
transformations, and named negative results.  Construction must not become a
second opaque world generator beside the one the player can inspect.

## The acceptance test

The game succeeds when attending to a place and changing it is as compelling
as crossing it; when the overhead reading makes the player want to ride, the
ride makes the player want to understand, and understanding makes one small
act of care seem necessary.

The generator makes a world no one designed.  The player does not replace it
with a design.  They live there long enough to notice what deserves to last.
